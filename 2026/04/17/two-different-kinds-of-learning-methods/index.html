debugging and learning difficult theory

Recently,I have been immersed in debugging our robot localization system.I’ve putting all my spare time into it ,and haven’t gone to the library in a while,because the first round of the competition is coming up,only two days left.As a result ,I am finding it hard to concentrate on my difficult major course.I am not sure why,but one reason might be that the debugging process doesn’t require careful thought and sustained focus,but taking class is the opposite.

Here’s a solid explanation for it.

low threshold and high reward work

Debugging is low threshold and high feedback.Every small fix gives you an instant result—-the robot moves,the error disappears.Your brain gets a little dopamine hit each time.
Studying difficult theory is the opposite— high threshold , delayed feedback.You sit with confusion for a long time before anything clicks.No instant reward.
When you spend days in “debug mode”,your brain gets reconditioned to expect fast feedback.Then when you sit down with a textbook,the lack of instant reward feels unbearable—-so your focus collapses.

how to deal with it

Ease back in with a short timer.

Separate your spaces.

If possible,don’t debug and study in the same place.Your brain associates environments with modes.Library=study mode.Lab=debug mode.

Accept the discomfort for the first 10 minutes.

the class Robotics Modeling and Control

It’s really hard for me to focus on my Robotics Modeling and Control lecture.It involves a lot of linear algebra and mechanics,and I’m not very familiar with either of them.On top of that,the teacher relies heavily on PPT slides,showing lots of mathematical formulas,and I can’t keep up with the pace of his class.I know the best way to learn is not to rely on the course alone,but to also carefully read and understand the textbook–but honestly,I am a little intimidated by that too.

I’ve been reading the Robotics Modeling and Control textbook.For the first and second reads,I just grasped the main framework of the chapter and figured out what the author was trying to do.As for the mathematics formulas,I still couldn’t work out how each formula was derived,it’s demanding work and would take a lot of time.Do you think it’s worth doing?

Grasping the framework first is exactly the right instinct.
